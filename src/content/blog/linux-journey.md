# From Mac Obsession to the Linux Rabbit Hole: A Journey I Never Planned

*"I couldn't afford a MacBook. But who said I couldn't experience it?"*

---

## It Started With a Bit of Envy

Around 2020, I often found myself sitting in front of my Windows laptop watching YouTube. And for some reason, every time I saw someone using a MacBook, the same thought kept popping up in my head:

*"Why can't my desktop look that clean?"*

Macs have this certain vibe that's hard to explain. It's not just the specs. It's the look and feel. The smooth dock, the fluid animations, the overall polish. Everything feels… premium.

Unfortunately, the price is premium too.

And at that time, my wallet was definitely not on the same level as my curiosity.

So I came up with an idea that, looking back, was a little ridiculous:

*"What if I just make my Windows look like a Mac?"*

---

## The YouTube Video That Changed Everything

One day, YouTube recommended a video about **a Linux distribution that looked like macOS**.

At first I clicked purely out of curiosity. But that video introduced me to something I hadn't really considered before: there were operating systems other than Windows.

And interestingly, the first Linux distro I tried wasn't Ubuntu.

It wasn't Mint either.

My first distro was **Deepin OS**.

Deepin is a Chinese Linux distribution, and honestly… it looked amazing. When I booted into it for the first time, it felt completely different from the Windows environment I was used to.

Clean. Smooth animations. A dock that looked very Mac-like.

At the time, I installed it as a **dual boot alongside Windows**, because I wasn't brave enough to abandon Windows entirely.

---

## The Reality Check: "Where Is Premiere Pro?"

The first few days were fun.

Everything felt new and exciting.

Until I needed actual software.

That's when reality hit.

I remember thinking:

*"Okay… where's Premiere Pro?"*
*"What about Photoshop?"*
*"Surely Illustrator exists here too, right?"*

Well… not really.

That's when I started discovering Linux alternatives:

* KDenLive or OpenShot for video editing
* GIMP for image editing
* Inkscape for vector graphics

My honest first impression?

*"These don't feel as powerful…"*

But after spending more time with them, I realized something important.

---

## I Misdiagnosed the Problem

At first, I blamed the applications.

But the more I explored, the more I realized the problem wasn't actually the software.

Applications like GIMP, Inkscape, and KDenLive are **surprisingly capable tools**. Many people use them for serious work.

The real issue I ran into was something people don't talk about as much:

**resources and tutorials.**

If you're using Premiere Pro and get stuck, you can open YouTube and instantly find dozens of tutorials explaining exactly what to do.

With Linux tools?

The tutorials exist… but there aren't nearly as many.

So when I thought the software was "not powerful enough," what was actually happening was much simpler:

**I didn't know how to use it properly.**

And there weren't many guides showing me how.

---

## Linux's Biggest Weakness

If I had to be honest, Linux still struggles in one major area.

**The software ecosystem.**

Many industry-standard tools simply aren't available on Linux. Adobe's entire suite is probably the most obvious example.

Of course, alternatives exist. In fact, there are many.

But there's an important nuance here: workflow.

For **personal use**, Linux alternatives are often more than enough.

But for **professional work with tight deadlines**, changing your workflow can introduce friction. Especially if everyone else in your team uses different software.

That doesn't mean Linux can't be used professionally. Many people do exactly that.

But it does mean there are trade-offs to consider.

---

## So… Is Linux Worth Trying?

In my opinion: **yes, absolutely.**

Especially when you look at how Windows has evolved over the past few years.

Ads are starting to appear in more places.
Bloatware keeps growing.
Updates sometimes feel forced.

Meanwhile, Linux has become far more beginner-friendly than it used to be.

Back in 2020, things still felt a little intimidating.

Today, there are distributions that are genuinely easy to start with.

The ones people usually recommend are:

* Linux Mint
* Pop!_OS
* Zorin OS
* Ubuntu

Linux no longer feels like an operating system reserved only for hackers or developers. It's become much more approachable.

---

## A Rabbit Hole I Don't Regret

The funny thing is, this whole journey started with something very simple.

I just wanted my Windows desktop to look like a Mac.

Instead, I fell into a much deeper world: open source software, unlimited customization, and a better understanding of how computers actually work.

I didn't end up with a Mac-like desktop.

But I gained something far more interesting:

**a sense of control over the machine I use every day.**

And honestly, that journey is still ongoing.
